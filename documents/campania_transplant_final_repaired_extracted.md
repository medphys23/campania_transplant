ABSTRACT

End-stage kidney disease (ESKD) represents a major public health burden in Italy, with significant regional disparities in incidence, prevalence, and outcomes. This comprehensive paper compares the integrated chronic kidney disease (CKD) care model implemented in Emilia-Romagna—the PIRP Project (Prevenzione Insufficienza Renale Progressiva)—with the current situation in Campania, a region characterized by higher ESKD burden and persistent healthcare inequities.

A central component of the PIRP model is the CT-PIRP (Classification Tree-PIRP) algorithm, a validated prognostic tool that stratifies CKD patients into seven distinct risk subgroups based on routinely collected clinical variables. This paper provides an in-depth analysis of the CT-PIRP algorithm, including its development, validation, clinical application, and the characteristics and outcomes of each risk subgroup.

Additionally, we present a comprehensive economic analysis comparing the costs of dialysis versus transplantation, with particular focus on the two highest-risk populations—patients with diabetes and hypertension—and the substantial cost savings achievable through pre-emptive transplantation strategies. Key economic findings include:

Annual dialysis costs: €49,000–€89,000 per patient

Annual post-transplant costs (after year 1): €15,000–€30,000 per patient

3-year cumulative savings per transplant: ~€66,000

Projected annual savings at full scale: €25.8 million (transplant expansion, PIRP incidence reduction, pre-emptive add-on)

Drawing on epidemiological data, intervention outcomes, cost-effectiveness analyses, and implementation science literature, we propose an evidence-based framework for adapting the PIRP model to Campania to reduce the incidence of ESKD, improve patient outcomes, and generate substantial healthcare cost savings.

PART I: INTRODUCTION

1. Background and Rationale

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) affects approximately 7% of the Italian adult population aged 35–79 years, with diabetes, hypertension, obesity, and cardiovascular disease representing the principal modifiable risk factors.[1] The burden of CKD and its progression to end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) is unevenly distributed across Italy. Southern regions, including Campania, experience higher incidence rates and poorer outcomes compared with northern regions such as Emilia-Romagna.[2] These regional disparities are largely attributable to differences in healthcare resources, care delivery structures, and socioeconomic conditions.[2]

The PIRP Project (Prevenzione Insufficienza Renale Progressiva), initiated in Emilia-Romagna in the early 2000s, represents a region-wide model of integrated CKD care that has been associated with measurable reductions in ESKD incidence and improved clinical outcomes. A central component of this program is the CT-PIRP algorithm, a classification tree–based prognostic tool that stratifies CKD patients into clinically meaningful risk groups using routinely collected variables. This risk stratification framework supports targeted intervention, resource prioritization, and structured care pathways.

1.1 Objectives of This Paper

The objectives of this paper are:

To describe the epidemiology of ESKD in Campania and compare it with Emilia-Romagna.

To provide a detailed analysis of the CT-PIRP algorithm and its seven risk subgroups.

To evaluate the economic burden of CKD in high-risk populations, particularly individuals with diabetes and hypertension.

To quantify the long-term cost differences between kidney transplantation and dialysis.

To assess the clinical and economic benefits of pre-emptive kidney transplantation.

To propose an evidence-based framework for adapting the PIRP model to the Campania healthcare system.

To project the expected clinical and economic impact of implementation.

PART II: EPIDEMIOLOGICAL CONTEXT

2. Global and National Burden of Chronic Kidney Disease

2.1 Global Epidemiology

Chronic kidney disease (CKD) represents a major and growing global public health challenge. In 2023, an estimated 788 million individuals aged 20 years and older were living with CKD worldwide, representing a substantial increase from 378 million in 1990 and 627 million in 2013.[3] Over the same period, the global age-standardised prevalence rose from 13.7% in 1990 to 14.2% in 2023.[3]

CKD is now recognised as a leading contributor to global morbidity and mortality. Deaths attributable to CKD have increased markedly over the past three decades, reflecting population ageing, rising prevalence of diabetes and hypertension, and improved survival from other chronic diseases that predispose to kidney dysfunction.

2.2 CKD Prevalence in Italy

In Italy, CKD prevalence is estimated at 7.05% (95% CI 6.48–7.65) among adults aged 35–79 years, a level generally lower than that reported in comparable population-based surveys outside Europe.[1] The stage distribution reflects a predominance of early disease:

Stages G1–G2 with albuminuria (A2–A3): 4.16% — early CKD with preserved glomerular filtration but evidence of kidney damage

Stages G3–G5: 2.89% — moderate to severe CKD

Early-stage disease: approximately 59% of the total CKD population

This distribution highlights the substantial proportion of individuals with early or asymptomatic disease, underscoring the importance of screening, risk stratification, and early intervention to prevent progression to advanced CKD and ESKD.

3. Prevalence of Major Risk Factors: Diabetes and Hypertension

Diabetes mellitus and hypertension are the principal modifiable drivers of chronic kidney disease (CKD) progression and represent the largest contributors to incident end-stage kidney disease (ESKD) in high-income countries. Accurate estimation of their population prevalence is therefore essential for projecting CKD burden and evaluating the potential impact of preventive interventions.

3.1 Hypertension in Italy

Hypertension is the most prevalent cardiovascular risk factor in Italy and a major determinant of CKD progression.[4][5]

Key epidemiological indicators include:

Overall prevalence: approximately 59.6% of adults

Sex distribution: 70.0% in men; 48.3% in women

Regional variation: higher prevalence in southern regions, including Campania

Awareness: approximately 59% of individuals with hypertension are aware of their condition

Control: only 25–30% achieve adequate blood pressure control

Longitudinal data from the CUORE Project (1998–2018) indicate that although awareness and treatment rates have improved over time, the overall prevalence of hypertension remains high. The burden is particularly pronounced among older adults and in southern Italy.[5]

3.2 Diabetes in Italy

The prevalence of diabetes in Italy has increased substantially over recent decades, reflecting demographic ageing, rising obesity, and sedentary lifestyles.[6]

Reported estimates vary depending on survey design and standardisation methods, but overall trends indicate sustained growth since the late twentieth century:

1980: approximately 2.5% of the population

2007: 15.3% in cardiovascular risk surveys (19.0% men, 11.2% women)

2013: 5.5% age-standardised prevalence, with higher rates in men (7.1%) than women (6.8%)

Regional variation: consistently higher prevalence in southern regions, including Campania

Type 2 diabetes: accounts for approximately 90% of all cases

Despite differences in measurement methodology across studies, diabetes prevalence in Italy has more than doubled since 1980, contributing substantially to the national CKD burden.[6]

3.3 Clustering of Cardiovascular Risk Factors

Cardiovascular and metabolic risk factors frequently coexist within individuals, amplifying the risk of kidney disease progression.[4]

Population-based data indicate:

Approximately 90% of Italian adults have more than one cardiovascular risk factor

About 84% present with between two and five concurrent risk factors

Southern regions, including Campania, show higher prevalence of obesity, hypertension, dyslipidaemia, and diabetes compared with northern regions

This clustering of cardiometabolic risk factors contributes to the disproportionately high burden of CKD and ESKD observed in southern Italy.

4. Epidemiology of End-Stage Kidney Disease in Campania

4.1 Population and Healthcare Context

Campania is the third most populous region in Italy, with approximately 5.8 million inhabitants. The regional healthcare system faces structural constraints that influence both the burden and outcomes of kidney disease.[2][7]

Key system-level challenges include:

Lower healthcare expenditure per capita compared with northern regions

Reduced availability of healthcare personnel, including physicians and nursing staff

Limited dialysis unit capacity

Higher prevalence of cardiometabolic risk factors in the general population

These structural and population-level factors contribute to increased demand for renal replacement therapy (RRT) and may limit timely access to specialized nephrology and transplant services.

4.2 Prevalence and Incidence of Kidney Replacement Therapy (2015–2018)

4.3 Healthcare Disparities Within Campania

Substantial inequalities in access to kidney replacement therapy and clinical outcomes exist within Campania, reflecting differences associated with both gender and geographic location.[7]

Gender disparities are evident in access to transplantation. Female patients are significantly less likely to receive a kidney transplant than male patients (hazard ratio [HR] 0.67–0.92). Despite lower access to transplantation, women experience mortality rates comparable to or higher than those of men, suggesting potential inequities in referral pathways, evaluation processes, or access to specialized care.

Geographic disparities also influence outcomes. Residence in suburban areas is associated with higher mortality risk (HR 1.03–1.24) and reduced likelihood of kidney transplantation (HR 0.65–0.98). In contrast, patients residing in urban centres benefit from greater proximity to nephrology services and transplant facilities, which likely facilitates earlier referral, more intensive follow-up, and improved access to specialized interventions.

Together, these findings indicate that access to kidney replacement therapy in Campania is shaped not only by clinical need but also by structural and demographic factors.

4.4 Role of Healthcare Resources

Regional variation in kidney disease outcomes in Italy is strongly associated with differences in healthcare system capacity. A 2025 population-level analysis demonstrated that disparities in kidney disease–related mortality are largely explained by variation in healthcare resources rather than intrinsic regional differences in disease burden.[2]

Key findings include:

Lower healthcare expenditure per capita is associated with increased kidney disease–related mortality

Reduced nursing workforce density correlates with poorer clinical outcomes

Limited dialysis unit capacity is associated with higher mortality

When regional-level resource indicators — including healthcare expenditure, nurse staffing, and dialysis unit availability — are incorporated into statistical models, previously observed regional differences in kidney disease–related mortality become non-significant. This finding suggests that healthcare infrastructure and resource allocation play a central role in determining patient outcomes.

These results underscore the importance of system-level capacity, workforce availability, and service accessibility in shaping the epidemiology of ESKD in Campania.

5.2 Implications

This comparison highlights fundamental differences in healthcare system organization that are likely to influence CKD progression and ESKD outcomes.

Emilia-Romagna operates a structured, population-based CKD management framework supported by a regional registry, integrated care pathways, and systematic risk stratification. These components enable early identification of high-risk patients, coordinated multidisciplinary care, and continuous monitoring of disease trajectories.

In contrast, Campania lacks several of the core organizational elements associated with effective CKD control:

Absence of a regional CKD registry to support population-level disease surveillance and planning

Lack of an integrated care pathway linking primary care and nephrology services

No systematic implementation of validated risk stratification tools such as CT-PIRP

More limited nephrology and transplant service capacity

These structural differences provide a plausible explanation for the divergent trends in dialysis incidence observed between the two regions and identify modifiable system-level targets for intervention.

PART III: THE PIRP PROJECT AND CT-PIRP ALGORITHM

6. The PIRP Project: Structure and Implementation

6.1 Historical Development

The PIRP Project (Prevenzione Insufficienza Renale Progressiva — Prevention of Progressive Renal Insufficiency) was established in Emilia-Romagna in the early 2000s as a region-wide initiative to address the growing burden of chronic kidney disease (CKD).[8] The program is formally endorsed and funded by the Emilia-Romagna Regional Health Authority and operates across all thirteen nephrology hospital units within the region.[9]

From its inception, PIRP was designed as a population-based disease management strategy integrating clinical care, epidemiological surveillance, and health system coordination.

6.2 Core Objectives

The PIRP Project was developed to achieve four principal objectives:[8][9]

Early identification — Detect CKD at an early stage before substantial loss of renal function occurs.

Delay disease progression — Implement evidence-based therapeutic interventions to slow decline in estimated glomerular filtration rate (eGFR).

Prevent complications — Reduce cardiovascular morbidity and mortality, the leading causes of death among patients with CKD.

Optimize transition to renal replacement therapy (RRT) — Minimize unplanned or emergency dialysis initiation through structured pre-RRT care planning.

6.3 Organizational Structure

The PIRP model is implemented through a coordinated, multi-level healthcare network that integrates governance, clinical services, and primary care.

Regional Level

Strategic oversight and funding by the Regional Health Authority

Centralized electronic CKD registry for population-level monitoring

Continuous quality monitoring and audit systems

Professional training and education programs

Hospital Level

Participation of all thirteen nephrology hospital units in the region

Dedicated outpatient clinics for CKD management

Multidisciplinary care teams

Standardized clinical protocols and care pathways

Primary Care Level

Structured training programs for general practitioners

Early detection and referral protocols

Shared-care management between primary care and nephrology services

Ongoing communication and feedback mechanisms across care levels

Together, these components form an integrated care framework linking population surveillance, risk stratification, and coordinated clinical management.

6.4 The PIRP Registry

A central operational component of the PIRP Project is the regional electronic CKD registry, which currently includes more than 38,000 patients and over 100,000 recorded nephrology visits.[8][9] The registry functions as the core infrastructure supporting population-based disease management and coordinated clinical care.

Its principal functions include:

Population health management: Identification of high-risk patients and prioritization of targeted interventions

Risk stratification: Data capture and processing to enable application of the CT-PIRP classification algorithm

Quality improvement: Continuous monitoring of clinical indicators with structured audit and feedback

Research support: Facilitation of epidemiological analyses and outcomes research

Resource planning: Data-driven allocation of healthcare personnel, services, and infrastructure

By integrating clinical data collection with risk stratification and performance monitoring, the registry enables continuous surveillance of CKD progression at both patient and population levels.

6.5 Clinical Care Pathway

The PIRP clinical care pathway is a structured, risk-based management framework that spans early detection, longitudinal disease management, and preparation for renal replacement therapy.

Initial Assessment

All patients undergo standardized baseline evaluation, including:

Comprehensive assessment of kidney function (estimated glomerular filtration rate and proteinuria)

Evaluation of comorbid conditions, including diabetes, hypertension, and cardiovascular disease

Laboratory profiling (serum phosphate, calcium, haemoglobin, lipid parameters)

Risk stratification using the CT-PIRP algorithm

Ongoing Management

Longitudinal care is individualized according to risk subgroup and focuses on slowing disease progression and reducing complications. Core components include:

Risk-stratified follow-up scheduling

Blood pressure optimization (target approximately 130/80 mmHg)

Glycaemic control in patients with diabetes

Renin–angiotensin–aldosterone system blockade for proteinuria reduction

Dietary counselling (protein, sodium, and phosphate management)

Anaemia management

Management of CKD–mineral and bone disorder

Cardiovascular risk reduction strategies

Transition Planning

For patients approaching advanced CKD, structured preparation for renal replacement therapy is initiated to minimize unplanned treatment initiation. This includes:

Timely creation of vascular access (arteriovenous fistula or graft)

Patient education regarding renal replacement therapy modalities

Evaluation for pre-emptive kidney transplantation when clinically appropriate

Planned initiation of dialysis to avoid emergency treatment starts

7. The CT-PIRP Algorithm: Development and Methodology

7.1 Rationale for Development

Large registry-based studies have identified numerous risk factors associated with chronic kidney disease (CKD) progression and transition to end-stage renal disease. However, traditional epidemiological analyses typically rely on cross-sectional or baseline measurements and often provide limited capacity to predict disease trajectories at the individual patient level.[8]

Many studies do not incorporate longitudinal assessment of kidney function decline and therefore offer limited prognostic resolution for clinical decision-making. The CT-PIRP model was developed to address this limitation by identifying combinations of demographic and clinical characteristics that discriminate among patients with differing rates of annual decline in glomerular filtration rate (GFR).[8] The objective was to generate a clinically interpretable risk stratification framework capable of supporting individualized prognostic assessment and targeted intervention.

7.2 Study Population and Design

The CT-PIRP model was derived from an observational retrospective cohort study based on data from the Emilia Romagna PIRP registry.[8]

Key design features include:

Data source: Regional PIRP registry

Enrolment period: July 2004 to June 2010

Inclusion criteria: Patients with at least four serum creatinine measurements to permit estimation of longitudinal GFR decline

Derivation cohort: 2,265 patients

Validation cohort: 2,051 subsequently enrolled patients, providing temporal external validation

This design enabled development of a prediction model based on real-world clinical data and independent assessment of model performance in a later patient cohort.

Statistical Methodology

Classification Tree Analysis (CTA) was used to identify clinically distinct subgroups of patients characterized by different rates of annual GFR decline. Predictor variables included demographic characteristics and laboratory measurements recorded at study entry.[8] CTA is a non-parametric recursive partitioning method that stratifies a population into increasingly homogeneous subgroups with respect to a specified outcome.

The analytical process involves:

Recursive partitioning: The algorithm sequentially divides the study population into subgroups based on predictor variables that best differentiate outcome values.

Optimal threshold identification: For each candidate predictor, the algorithm determines the cut-off value that maximizes separation between groups with differing GFR decline.

Hierarchical decision structure: The resulting model is represented as a branching tree in which each node corresponds to a decision rule.

Terminal node classification: Each patient is assigned to a mutually exclusive terminal subgroup defined by a specific combination of predictor values.

Advantages of Classification Tree Analysis

CTA offers several methodological advantages in clinical risk stratification:

Captures non-linear relationships and higher-order interactions among predictors

Produces transparent and clinically interpretable decision rules

Relies on variables routinely available in clinical practice

Does not require parametric assumptions regarding variable distributions

Empirically identifies optimal decision thresholds from observed data

These properties make CTA particularly suitable for translating complex clinical data into actionable risk classification.

Several limitations should be considered when interpreting the model:

Regional derivation: Developed in Emilia-Romagna; external populations may require recalibration or validation.

Moderate discriminatory performance: C-statistics between 0.68 and 0.71 indicate acceptable but not high predictive accuracy.

Baseline-only classification: Risk stratification is based on initial measurements and does not incorporate longitudinal change.

Limited predictor set: Potentially relevant variables, such as underlying CKD aetiology or haemoglobin levels, are not included.

Binary proteinuria variable: Degree of proteinuria is not quantified, limiting granularity of risk estimation.

11.1 Integration into Clinical Workflow

The CT-PIRP algorithm is integrated into the PIRP registry electronic system, enabling automatic risk stratification at each patient encounter.

Fully Autonomous Clinical Decision Workflow

Automated Data Ingestion: Continuous capture of laboratory results and patient demographics directly from source systems (LIS, EHR, scanned documents). OCR and structured data extraction operate in real time with automatic validation, normalization, and error detection.

Algorithmic Patient Stratification: A rule-based and model-driven classification engine assigns each patient to one of seven predefined subgroups immediately upon data availability. No manual review is required unless system integrity thresholds are exceeded.

Real-Time Risk Quantification and Visualization: A dynamic dashboard updates automatically, displaying risk tier, predicted outcomes, confidence metrics, and trend trajectories without user input.

Autonomous Clinical Recommendation Generation: An evidence-based decision engine generates subgroup-specific management pathways, monitoring protocols, and intervention priorities, and automatically records them in the patient file.

Self-Executing Follow-Up Scheduling: The system calculates optimal monitoring intervals based on risk profile and automatically schedules follow-up appointments, sends reminders, and queues required investigations.

Closed-Loop Monitoring and Recalibration: All incoming data continuously trigger re-evaluation of classification, risk level, and management plan. Any change automatically updates recommendations and scheduling.

Exception Handling and System Governance: Only predefined anomalies (e.g., data conflicts, model uncertainty above threshold, or system faults) generate alerts for human oversight. All standard cases proceed without intervention.

12.2 Model Methods

This economic evaluation employs a deterministic cohort budget-impact framework to estimate direct medical cost consequences of kidney transplantation expansion and CKD progression reduction from the perspective of the Campania regional healthcare payer.

Model Perspective and Objective

The analysis estimates direct healthcare cost consequences associated with changes in renal replacement therapy utilization, including dialysis and kidney transplantation, from the perspective of the Campania regional healthcare payer.

Time Horizon and Discounting

The base-case time horizon is 10 years. This horizon was selected to align with the implementation scenarios and cumulative savings projections presented in the economic section. Discounted and undiscounted results may differ depending on the assumptions applied; the narrative interpretation in this proposal focuses primarily on undiscounted budget impact.

Model Structure

The economic model is a deterministic cohort budget-impact model with three components:

transplant expansion, modelled as additional transplants occurring each year

reduced progression to end-stage kidney disease under integrated CKD care;

the incremental benefit of pre-emptive transplantation.

These components are treated as analytically distinct to reduce double counting.

Cost Definitions and Program Savings

Dialysis is treated as the reference-cost modality. Transplantation is associated with higher costs in the initial year and lower maintenance costs thereafter. Program-level savings therefore arise from repeated annual cohorts of additional transplant recipients, together with fewer patients entering dialysis because of slower CKD progression. The fully rendered parameter set and equations are provided in Appendix X.

Sensitivity Analysis

Sensitivity analysis is presented as one-way variation around key epidemiological and cost assumptions, including dialysis cost, transplant year-1 cost, transplant maintenance cost, transplant activity, and the assumed reduction in incident ESKD under PIRP-style care. The results should be interpreted as scenario-based estimates rather than precise forecasts.

15. Cost Savings from Slowed CKD Progression

15.1 Clinical Impact of Delaying Dialysis Initiation

Integrated CKD care can reduce the rate of kidney function decline and postpone the onset of ESKD, thereby delaying or avoiding dialysis initiation. Because maintenance dialysis carries substantial annual cost, even modest delays in progression can have important budget implications.[8][9]

Within the CT-PIRP framework, the largest potential clinical benefit is expected in higher-risk groups, particularly patients with proteinuric CKD and faster baseline decline. The patient-level examples in this section are illustrative and are used to explain the mechanism by which earlier intervention may translate into reduced need for dialysis.

For consistency, the headline economic estimates in this proposal do not aggregate individual patient delay-based savings directly. Instead, they model the system-level effect as a reduction in the number of patients entering dialysis each year. This approach is more conservative and avoids double counting.

15.2 Population-Level Economic Impact in Campania

At health-system level, slower CKD progression is represented as a proportional reduction in incident ESKD. Under this structure, fewer patients initiate dialysis each year, generating avoided expenditure for the regional payer.

The base-case scenario applies an incidence-reduction parameter to the Campania ESKD burden and converts the avoided incident cases into annual dialysis cost savings. Ten-year cumulative savings are then derived by extending this annual effect over the model horizon.

These estimates should be read as conservative population-level budget-impact projections. They are intended to capture the aggregate effect of integrated care, risk-factor control, and disease-modifying therapy without overstating the certainty of any single intervention effect.

16. Diabetes and Hypertension: Targeted Economic Impact

16.1 Diabetic CKD Population

Diabetes is a major contributor to CKD progression and kidney failure and is therefore a major driver of dialysis expenditure in Campania.[8][9]

Disease-modifying therapies, including SGLT2 inhibitors in appropriate patients, can reduce progression to kidney failure in diabetic CKD. In this proposal, these effects are not modelled as an independent savings stream; instead, they are incorporated within the broader CKD progression-reduction module described in Section 15.

16.2 Hypertensive CKD Population

Hypertension is another major contributor to CKD progression and to the long-term burden of ESKD. Better blood pressure control, earlier nephrology referral, and sustained monitoring are therefore central implementation levers within an integrated care model.

As with diabetes-related interventions, the economic effect of improved hypertension management is captured indirectly through the incidence-reduction framework rather than modelled as a separate additive component. This prevents double counting and keeps the budget-impact model internally consistent.

Taken together, diabetes and hypertension represent the principal biological mechanisms through which integrated CKD care can reduce incident ESKD in Campania.

17. Summary: Total Economic Impact

17.1 Limitations of the Economic Model

Several methodological and structural limitations should be considered when interpreting the economic projections.

Parameter uncertainty: model outputs depend on cost inputs and epidemiological assumptions, including annual dialysis cost, transplant costs in year 1 and maintenance years, and incident ESKD estimates. These parameters may vary across regions and over time.

Absence of cost-effectiveness analysis: The model estimates budget impact only. Health outcomes such as quality-adjusted life years (QALYs), survival gains, and patient utility are not incorporated.

Simplified survival and graft dynamics: Mortality, graft failure, and return to dialysis are not modelled explicitly. Transplanted patients are assumed to maintain stable graft function over the 10-year time horizon.

Incident-flow implementation assumptions: program-level savings depend on the annual number of additional transplants achieved in practice. Real-world implementation may involve gradual scale-up, workforce limitations, or capacity constraints.

Integrated CKD progression modelling: The PIRP incidence-reduction parameter represents the aggregate effect of risk-factor control and disease-modifying therapy. Diabetes and hypertension management effects are incorporated within this parameter to avoid double counting.

18. Pre-Emptive Kidney Transplantation: Evidence and Benefits

18.1 Definition and Guideline Recommendations

Pre-emptive kidney transplantation (PKT) is defined as kidney transplantation performed before initiation of dialysis therapy.

The Kidney Disease: Improving Global Outcomes (KDIGO) 2020 Clinical Practice Guideline recommends pre-emptive transplantation with a living donor as the preferred treatment for transplant-eligible patients with advanced chronic kidney disease (CKD), supported by high-certainty evidence (Grade 1A).[15]

In adults, transplantation is generally planned when kidney failure is anticipated within the near term and a donor work-up can be completed safely, with earlier transplantation considered in the presence of uraemic symptoms or other clinical indications.[15]

18.2 Clinical Outcomes of Pre-Emptive Transplantation

A systematic review of 14 cohort studies demonstrated that pre-emptive transplantation is associated with superior graft outcomes compared with transplantation after dialysis initiation.[14]

A paediatric systematic review and meta-analysis including 22 studies (n = 22,622) similarly reported better graft-related outcomes and lower acute rejection rates among children undergoing pre-emptive transplantation compared with transplantation after dialysis exposure.[10]

18.3 Impact of Dialysis Vintage

Duration of dialysis prior to transplantation (“dialysis vintage”) is an important determinant of post-transplant outcomes.

A nationwide Korean cohort study of living donor kidney transplant recipients (n = 3,392) found that longer dialysis exposure before transplantation was associated with worse post-transplant outcomes than pre-emptive transplantation; short dialysis exposure (≤6 months) was associated with outcomes closer to those seen with pre-emptive transplantation.[11,12,13]

A large Austrian registry study (n = 6,979) likewise showed that prolonged dialysis vintage before transplantation was associated with higher post-transplant mortality than shorter pre-transplant dialysis duration.[11,12,13]

These findings indicate a dose–response relationship between dialysis exposure and adverse transplant outcomes.

18.4 Optimal Timing for Transplant Referral

Timely referral for transplant evaluation is essential to enable pre-emptive transplantation. KDIGO guidance recommends early referral of transplant candidates to allow sufficient time for donor identification, evaluation, listing, and surgical planning.[15] The 2024 KDIGO CKD guideline further emphasizes risk-based referral using validated prediction tools, such as the Kidney Failure Risk Equation (KFRE), to identify patients with a high probability of kidney failure within 2 years.[16] Early risk stratification improves treatment planning and increases the likelihood of successful pre-emptive transplantation.

19. Team-Based Integrated Care: KDIGO Framework

19.1 Core Components of Multidisciplinary CKD Care

The KDIGO 2024 Clinical Practice Guideline recommends that patients with chronic kidney disease have access to a patient-centred multidisciplinary care team to support comprehensive disease management and preparation for kidney replacement therapy.[16]

Core components of multidisciplinary CKD care include:

Nutritional assessment and dietary counselling

Medication review and optimization

Education regarding kidney replacement therapy (KRT) modalities

Counselling on transplant eligibility and options

Planning for dialysis access creation

Ethical, psychological, and social support

This integrated model addresses both medical and non-medical determinants of disease progression and treatment readiness.

19.2 Structural Elements of Effective CKD Care Models

Successful CKD care programs share several essential structural elements that enable coordinated, longitudinal disease management.[16]

Navigation systems
Structured referral pathways ensuring timely identification of high-risk patients and coordinated transitions between primary care and specialist services.

Education programs
Comprehensive patient education covering CKD progression, treatment options (including dialysis and transplantation), and conservative management strategies.

Surveillance protocols
Standardized monitoring of kidney function, cardiovascular risk, and CKD-related complications, including laboratory assessment and preventive care such as vaccination.

Clinical management frameworks
Support for self-management, lifestyle modification, pharmacologic therapy, and psychosocial care delivered through coordinated multidisciplinary input.

Integrated communication systems
Continuous information exchange among patients, primary care providers, and specialist teams to ensure continuity and consistency of care.

19.3 Evidence Supporting Multidisciplinary CKD Care

Evidence from diverse healthcare systems demonstrates that coordinated, team-based care improves delivery of guideline-recommended CKD management and reduces adverse outcomes. The Kidney CHAMP randomized clinical trial showed that a multifaceted population health management intervention improved adherence to guideline-based CKD care. The intervention combined electronic health record–based risk identification with coordinated nephrology input, pharmacist-led medication management, and structured patient education.[14]

Population-level program implementation has also demonstrated substantial impact. The Indian Health Service reduced diabetes-related ESKD incidence by 54% following implementation of systematic CKD surveillance and management strategies, including routine eGFR reporting, annual urine albumin monitoring, renin–angiotensin system blockade, and culturally tailored patient education.[14] These findings indicate that integrated care models can substantially reduce progression to kidney failure when applied at scale.

PART VII: CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS

22. Summary of Key Findings

This comprehensive analysis demonstrates that:

Campania faces a significant CKD burden with ESKD incidence of 200 PMP, higher than the Italian average, driven primarily by diabetes and hypertension.

The Emilia-Romagna PIRP model provides a practical reference framework for integrated CKD care, with large-scale regional implementation and reported improvements in care delivery and selected outcomes.

The CT-PIRP algorithm effectively stratifies CKD patients into seven risk subgroups using five routinely available clinical variables, enabling targeted resource allocation.

Highest-risk patients (Subgroups 1 and 3) require intensive intervention, while lowest-risk patients (Subgroups 4 and 6) can be managed primarily in primary care.

Pre-emptive transplantation is associated with superior graft and patient survival and should be prioritized for eligible patients.

The economic analysis suggests potential annual savings of approximately €25.8 million at full implementation, driven by transplant expansion, reduced progression to ESKD, and the incremental benefit of pre-emptive transplantation. These estimates should be interpreted as model-based projections rather than guaranteed savings.

23. Recommendations

For Regional Health Authorities:

Establish a regional CKD registry with integrated CT-PIRP risk stratification

Fund multidisciplinary CKD clinics at all nephrology units

Implement quality monitoring and audit systems

Develop primary care training and shared care protocols

For Nephrology Units:

Adopt CT-PIRP algorithm for all CKD patients

Prioritize Subgroups 1 and 3 for intensive intervention

Establish timely transplant referral pathways

Ensure planned vascular access creation for high-risk patients

For Primary Care:

Screen high-risk populations (diabetics, hypertensives) for CKD

Refer patients with eGFR ≤30 mL/min/1.73 m² to nephrology

Participate in shared care for low-risk CKD patients

Optimize evidence-based renoprotective therapy for diabetic CKD patients, including SGLT2 inhibitors where clinically appropriate

For Transplant Programs:

Increase pre-emptive transplantation to >20% of all kidney transplants

Streamline living donor evaluation

Expand deceased donor procurement

Target transplant rate of 35 PMP (matching Emilia-Romagna)

24. Conclusion

Implementation of an integrated CKD care model in Campania, informed by the Emilia-Romagna PIRP experience and incorporating structured risk stratification, could improve patient outcomes and reduce avoidable healthcare expenditure. Systematic identification of high-risk patients, targeted multidisciplinary intervention, and earlier transplant pathway activation may reduce ESKD incidence, decrease unplanned dialysis starts, and improve readiness for kidney replacement therapy.

The available evidence supports phased regional implementation with formal monitoring and evaluation. The principal requirements are governance, registry infrastructure, aligned referral pathways, and sustained organizational commitment.
